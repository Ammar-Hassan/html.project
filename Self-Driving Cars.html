Self-Driving Cars

Three things you should know about self-driving cars: -

  1. In late 2015, Tesla Motors made a software update to its vehicles around the world. This company already Install sensors, cameras, GPS and controls onboard the cars to be able to drive autonomous, although, humans are being in the driver’s seat ready to take over if needed. Within weeks, a crew of drivers climbed into a Model (S) in Los Angeles and travel to New York over only two days, the car steering itself 96% of the way. Tesla founder expects that his electric cars will be entirely self-driving by the end of 2020. Mainline carmakers from General Motors to Mercedes-Benz have also promised to sell autonomous vehicles in the next few years. Born-again evangelists of self-driving cars include some of the greatest names in the business, such as William Clay Ford Jr., the executive chairman of the company and Toyota Motor \ Corp. president Akio Toyoda Maryl, Tennessee, Georgia and Illinois have legalized self-driving cars, and at least 13 more states are mulling similar laws.

The computer is better driver than a human because it better at keeping its eyes on other drivers, better at maintaining a steady driving speed and that way maximizing fuel efficiency, better at parsing GPS data, weather data and traffic data. It doesn’t get distracted by a spouse, kids or anything. It doesn’t sneak a glimpse at Snapchat or WhatsApp. The computer couldn’t blink even if it wants. Never persuades itself that harmful weed improves its driving. Asking directions is the favorite activity for the computer, the computer never falls asleep.

  3. The economic and safety effects will be very good. There are many statistics to prove that driving puts others’ lives at risk. Self-driving cars and unmanned drones will probably join digital surveillance that Distinguishes our times. Freedom vs. security, the problem is transition from human drivers to more-skillful computers. And because the gulf between human and machine is very big the next step after making driverless cars legal will be making them mandatory. Who will be responsible for mistakes? How should computers make life and death decisions? Such questions may contort ethicists and lawyers for next years. But all revolutions involve disruption and I think this one will create more than it destroys. Over the world, there are about 1.3 million traffic fatalities every year, according to the World Health Organization. On the other hand, 94% of road accidents are the fault of drivers.
